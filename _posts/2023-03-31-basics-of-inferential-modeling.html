Basics of Inferential Modeling¶

Inferential modeling is a statistical approach used to draw conclusions about a population based on a sample of data. The Statsmodels library in Python provides tools for building and analyzing inferential models, including regression models, time series models, and other advanced statistical techniques.

As an exercise, I will be using the famous Boston dataset.

The Boston dataset is a built-in dataset in Statsmodels that contains information about housing in the Boston area. The dataset contains 506 observations and 14 columns:

  • crim: per capita crime rate by town
  • zn: proportion of residential land zoned for lots over 25,000 sq.ft.
  • indus: proportion of non-retail business acres per town
  • chas: Charles River dummy variable (= 1 if tract bounds river; 0 otherwise)
  • nox: nitric oxides concentration (parts per 10 million)
  • rm: average number of rooms per dwelling
  • age: proportion of owner-occupied units built prior to 1940
  • dis: weighted mean of distances to five Boston employment centres
  • rad: index of accessibility to radial highways
  • tax: full-value property-tax rate per $10,000
  • ptratio: pupil-teacher ratio by town
  • black: 1000(Bk - 0.63)^2 where Bk is the proportion of blacks by town
  • lstat: lower status of the population (%)
  • medv: median value of owner-occupied homes in $1000s

The goal in this exercise is to fit a linear regression model to infer properties about the relationships between the response variable medv and input variables.

Immediately, we observe that indus and age have very large p-values that are greater than 0.05 which the conventionally required level of significance. As such, they are statistically insignificant and it may be permissible to exclude them from our model.

By definition, p-value represents the smallest level of significance where we can still reject the null hypothesis, given the observed test statistic. That is, under the null hypothesis, p-value tells us the probability of obtaining an effect at least as large as the one observed in our sample. To consider a variable to be statistically significant, the p-value has to be less than the required level of significance.

Secondly, we note that the adjusted R-squared value 0.734, which tell us that 73.4% of the variation in medv can be explained by our model.

By definition, R-squared is the coefficient of determination, which is the proportion of the variation in the dependent variable that is predictable from the independent variable(s). It is a measure of goodness of fit of a model, and a statistical measure of how well the regression predictions approximate the real data points.

OLS Assumptions¶

In practice before conducting inferential modeling, it's important to check that the assumptions of linear regression are met.

  • Linearity in Parameters
  • Random Sampling (assumed to be true from data collected)
  • Independence (No Perfect Collinearity among Covariates)
  • Exogeneity
  • Homoscedasticity (constant variance) of Errors
  • Normality of Residuals

Linearity in Parameters¶

This assumption requires that the relationship between the independent variables and the dependent variable is linear, which means that a change in the independent variable will result in a proportional change in the dependent variable. If the relationship is not linear, then the model may not accurately capture the relationship between the variables and the model's predictions may be biased or unreliable.

If there is evidence of non-linearity in the relationship between the independent variables and the dependent variable, we may need to transform the data or use non-linear regression models, such as polynomial regression or spline regression, to capture the non-linear relationship.

To check for linearity, we can create scatter plots of the dependent variable medv against each independent variable. If the points in the scatter plot form a straight line, it suggests that a linear relationship is present.

If we observe an non-linear relationship that is obvious, we improve our model by applying the necessary variable transformation to achieve linearity.

Independence (No Perfect Collinearity among Covariates)¶

If an independent variable is an exact linear combination of other independent variables, we say that the model suffers from perfect collinearity, and it cannot be estimated by OLS. In reality, a lot of data is naturally correlated. Multicollinearity is a problem in regression analysis since we can't hold correlated coefficients constant. Additionally, it increases the standard error of the coefficients, which results in them potentially showing as statistically insignificant when they might actually be significant.

To check for multicollinearity, we can either visually inspect a correlation heatmap, or examine the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) of each predictor. Variables with correlation coefficients greater than 0.7 or less than -0.7 are typically considered to be highly correlated, and a VIF value of 1 indicates no multicollinearity, while values greater than 1 indicate increasing levels of multicollinearity. VIF values exceeding 5 are usually indicate high multicollinearity.

Exogeneity¶

The exogeneity assumption is such that the dependent variable does not causally affect its independent variables. Its formal definition is the zero conditional mean of errors, that is to say that the error term is uncorrelated with the regressors.

The presence of omitted-variable bias (OVB) violates this assumption, and causes the OLS estimators to be biased and inconsistent. To address OVB, we can consider introducing proxy variables or use instrumental variable (IV) techniques such as the two-stage least squares regression. Proxy variables, on the other hand, are variables that are used as a substitute for a variable of interest when the variable of interest is not available or cannot be measured directly. On the other hand, instrumental variables are variables that are correlated with the independent variable of interest, but are not affected by the error term in the regression equation.

To check for exogeneity, we can construct a residual plot. If the residuals are randomly scattered around the zero line, it suggests that the independent variables are exogenous.

Homoscedasticity (constant variance) of Errors¶

To formally test for homoscedasticity, we can use the Breusch-Pagan test. The test involves regressing the squared residuals on the independent variables and then testing whether the slope coefficient of the independent variable is significantly different from zero. If the p-value of the test is less than the required level of significance (0.05), then we can reject the null hypothesis of homoscedasticity and conclude that there is heteroscedasticity.

In the presence of heteroscedasticity, we can opt to use robust standard errors instead (given that our sample is sufficiently large).

In [ ]:
from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import het_breuschpagan

# Breusch-Pagan test
_, pval, _, _ = het_breuschpagan(model.resid, X)
print('Breusch-Pagan test p-value: ', pval)

Breusch-Pagan test p-value:  6.265430719876013e-09

We reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is in fact heteroscedasticity present. To address this, we can use the White or Huber-White estimator of the covariance matrix, which provides consistent standard errors that are robust to heteroscedasticity. We can specify this estimator using cov_type='HC3' as the argument in the fit() method of the statsmodels library.

Normality of Residuals¶

If the residuals are normally distributed, the points on the Q-Q plot should fall on or near the straight line. If the points deviate from the straight line, this indicates that the residuals are not normally distributed. To formally test for normality, we can use the Shapiro-Wilk test. If the p-value is less than the significance level (0.05), then we can reject the null hypothesis of normality and conclude that the residuals are not normally distributed.

Interpreting Regression Coefficients¶

From our regression coefficients, we can observe the effect each independent variable has on medv. For example, a unit increase in per capita crime rate by town would decrease the median value of owner-occupied homes by $118.5 on average, ceteris paribus.

Comparing between Models¶

AIC and BIC scores are two criterions we use to compare 2 models. AIC and BIC combine a term reflecting how well the model fits the data with a term that penalizes the model in proportion to its number of parameters. That is, the model with lower AIC and BIC scores is considered to be better.
